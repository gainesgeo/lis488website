Research

Through my undergraduate years, master's program, and early career, I've engaged in many research projects. These various projects have developed both my skills and interests and indicate a direction for my future research.

Washington & Lee University

Honors Thesis, "Reading Like a Mother: A New Approach to the Medieval Griselda Tale" (2022)

To earn the distinction of graduating with Honors from the Washington and Lee University English department, students are required to write a 60-80 page thesis on the topic of their choosing under the direction of a faculty advisor. My senior thesis also met the requirements for a capstone project for my secondary major in Medieval & Renaissance Studies. In this thesis, I argue for a new reading of the Griselda tale that priveleges her motherhood over her wifehood. Though translators try to conceal Griselda's motherhood and scholars try to avoid it, motherhood permeates the text. In creating a new reading of the Griselda tale, I relocate Griselda's motherhood and recenter the story on a "motherhood plot," seeing a story less about a wife surviving the cruelty of her husband and more about a mother who dearly loves her children.

The full text of this thesis is available online through the Washington and Lee University digital archive.

Florence As It Was (2021-2022)

Florence As It Was is a digital humanities project that uses point-cloud and photogrammetry models to digitally reconstruct the city of Florence as it would have appeared in the medieval period. When I joined the team, I started to contribute to the project by researching, writing, and editing short essays of 500 words about art, architecture, and culture for encyclopedic entries. In my second year with the project, I also developed infrastructure for a database of all artworks in Florence between 1200 and 1500 and entered several hundred pieces of data. You can access the database, models, encyclopedia, and much more on the FLAW website.

Summer Research Scholars (2020, 2021)

Through the Summer Research Scholars program at my undergraduate university, I was able to participate in two distinct research projects with Washington & Lee faculty. First, in the summer of 2020, I assisted a Classics professor with preliminary research for an e-book about the performance history of Antigone. This project was in collaboration with students at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln and the Archive of Performances of Greek and Roman Drama at Oxford University.

The following summer of 2021, I worked with a History professor on paleographical research for her forthcoming book on theories of speech in medieval Italy. I transcribed the Latin manuscript of fourteenth-century Florentine court records of the judicial official podesta and translated relevant data about nearly 100 crimes and entered the data into a spreadsheet for future mapping.
